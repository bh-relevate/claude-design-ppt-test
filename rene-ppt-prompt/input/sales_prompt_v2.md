# GAME-BASED HCP EDUCATION: MAKING THE CASE
## How Healthy Discussions Is Changing the Way Clinicians Prepare for Vaccine Hesitancy Conversations

**Prepared for:** Medical Affairs, Commercial, and Learning & Development Leaders
**Basis:** Independent moderated research with Healthcare Professionals across family medicine, internal medicine, pediatrics, and urgent care

---

## SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Vaccine hesitancy remains one of the most persistent and time-consuming challenges in modern clinical practice — and HCPs have, until now, lacked tools equal to the task. Independent research into the Healthy Discussions game found that HCPs with genuine learning opportunity reported confidence gains of up to **2 full points on a 7-point scale** after a single gameplay session, gains they directly attributed to the game's hesitancy-identification framework and clinical data content. More broadly, **9 out of 11 clinicians rated their hesitation to engage with game-based medical learning at 3 out of 7 or below** — indicating that HCP receptivity to this format is not a barrier. Among those who found the game most relevant to their practice, likelihood to recommend it to a colleague averaged above **9 out of 10**. The research establishes a clear case: when game-based learning is built on real clinical data, designed for HCPs, and deployed to the right audience, it produces measurable outcomes that traditional formats have not delivered.

---

## SECTION 2: THE HCP CHALLENGE

HCPs are not struggling to talk about COVID vaccines because they lack conviction. They are struggling because the conversations are complex, time-constrained, and emotionally loaded — and the tools available to prepare them have not kept pace with the challenge.

In the research sessions, clinicians described a daily reality that traditional educational formats are not addressing:

**The conversations are already happening — repeatedly.** One participant described COVID vaccine hesitancy as something that came up *"today, ten minutes ago."* Another said she no longer raises the topic proactively because so many patients have already made up their minds, and she lacks the conversational tools to change them.

**Patient resistance is not monolithic — but HCPs are being trained as if it were.** Clinicians described hesitant patients who had fundamentally different concerns: fear of adverse events, distrust of public figures, social media misinformation, and suspicion of government involvement. Most said their training had given them one or two general responses — not a framework for identifying which concern they were actually dealing with.

**The available tools are falling short.** A senior family medicine physician described CDC information sheets as *"so boring and wordy that they're not convincing."* An internal medicine physician said journal articles were useful for building personal knowledge but translated poorly into patient conversations. Across the group, HCPs described a gap between the information they had access to and the confidence they needed in the room.

The format gap is real. The appetite for something better is clear.

## SECTION 4: WHAT THE RESEARCH SHOWS

---

### Finding 1: The Game Builds the Conversational Skills HCPs Need Most

The game taught HCPs a clinical skill that textbooks and webinars have failed to deliver efficiently: the ability to identify *why* a specific patient is hesitant before choosing a response. The concern-identification framework — which maps patient statements to hesitancy categories such as adverse event fear, public figure distrust, and social media misinformation — was described as immediately transferable to real patient conversations.

HCPs with lower baseline confidence in vaccine hesitancy discussions reported the clearest gains — up to 2 points on a 7-point confidence scale — after completing the game. Those gains were explicitly attributed to the game's content: the hesitancy framework gave them a new diagnostic tool, and the statistical data gave them the evidence to back it up.

*"A family medicine physician in a mid-career practice described going from a confidence score of 4 to 6 specifically for challenging pediatric vaccine conversations — directly attributing the gain to what she learned in the game's data and dialogue modules."*

---

### Finding 2: The Game Delivers Clinical Data That HCPs Will Use at the Point of Care

The game's statistical content — COVID hospitalization rates, mortality data, and adverse event frequencies by age group — was the most consistently valued element across participant types. This content filled a real gap: HCPs knew that these conversations required evidence, but few had precise, readily retrievable numbers.

Multiple clinicians described specific behavioral intentions after engaging with the data: one planned to write the key statistics down on a card to carry in her lab coat. Another described the data as giving her *"information to help the conversation"* that she hadn't had access to before. The game does not just teach HCPs what to say — it gives them the specific evidence to say it with.

Among participants who completed the game experience, learning effectiveness ratings ranged from 6 to 7 out of 7, driven primarily by the statistical content layer.

---

### Finding 3: HCPs Are Receptive to This Format — Across Experience Levels and Specialties

Across physicians and physician assistants, across family medicine, internal medicine, and urgent care, across early-career and senior practitioners — **9 out of 11 participants rated their hesitation to engage with game-based medical learning at 3 out of 7 or below.**

This is not a format that needs to overcome reluctance. The baseline attitude is curiosity and openness. Clinicians who grew up on video games described the format as a natural fit for learning. Clinicians who did not described it as a welcome relief from journal articles and compliance modules. Across both groups, game-based medical education was seen as a format that fits into the gaps in an HCP's day — not one that creates additional burden.

*"A physician assistant with 10 years in urgent care and primary care rated her hesitation at 1 out of 7 and described her experience as: 'This is like a real conversation. This is exactly what happens.'"*

---

### Finding 4: The Game Earns High Recommendation Intent Among Clinicians Who Engage With It

Among HCPs who found the game relevant to their clinical practice, recommendation scores averaged above 9 out of 10. These were not passive endorsements — participants described specific peer contexts in which they would share the game: with early-career colleagues navigating their first years of vaccine hesitancy conversations, within residency program orientations, and across multidisciplinary care teams where multiple staff members engage with hesitant patients.

This level of peer advocacy has direct implications for reach. An HCP who recommends the game to colleagues becomes a trusted distribution channel — far more credible than a pharma email or a website banner.

---

### Finding 5: Game-Based Learning Addresses a Format Gap That Traditional CME Has Left Open

Participants described existing educational tools — journal articles, CDC fact sheets, webinars, static PDFs — as informative but not engaging, and engaging but not informative. The game sits in a position that existing formats do not occupy: it is simultaneously data-rich, interactive, and designed for the specific clinical context HCPs are navigating.

A senior internal medicine physician who described journal articles as *"putting you to sleep"* gave the game's statistical module a 10 out of 10 for enjoyment — then described how she would use the content in her next patient encounter. The format differentiation is real, and HCPs articulate it clearly when asked.

## SECTION 6: THE OPPORTUNITY

### A Format With Proven HCP Demand — and Portfolio-Wide Potential

The research establishes more than a single product's value. It establishes that game-based medical learning is a format that HCPs actively want, across specialties and career stages. When participants were asked what other clinical topics they would want in a game-based format, treatments were selected by 10 out of 11, disease states by 8 out of 11, and pharmaceuticals by 7 out of 11. This is near-universal interest in a format — not just interest in this game.

For a pharmaceutical company with a portfolio of products and a standing need to educate HCPs on mechanism of action, treatment protocols, and disease state management, that interest represents an education channel, not a one-time campaign.

---

### Reaching HCPs Where They Are, When They Are Ready

The research revealed a consistent behavioral pattern: HCPs want to engage with educational content during protected time — designated administrative blocks, lunch breaks, or evenings when clinical demands are complete. Game-based learning is uniquely suited to this pattern because it is self-paced, accessible on mobile devices, and completable in segments. Unlike a one-hour webinar that requires calendar commitment, a well-designed game can deliver substantive learning in the fifteen minutes between patients or during a Wednesday afternoon administrative half-day.

Participants described exactly these use cases: one family medicine physician described her Wednesday administrative time as her primary window for continuing education. A physician assistant working 12-hour urgent care shifts described doing education during the unpredictable downtime between patients. These are windows that webinars and journal reading cannot easily fill — but a mobile-first interactive game can.

---

### Competitive Differentiation Against Traditional CME

Traditional CME formats have a completion problem. HCPs attend webinars while charting. They start PDF modules and close the tab. They read journal articles at varying levels of attention. The research found that game-based learning produced enjoyment scores of 8–10 out of 10 for the clinical data modules — the educational core — not just for entertainment elements. An HCP who is engaged at a 9/10 level is retaining content. An HCP who is disengaged in a webinar is not.

Enjoyment is not a vanity metric in HCP education — it is a proxy for attention, retention, and completion. The data here shows that when the content is clinically grounded and the format is respectful of HCPs' time and intelligence, enjoyment and learning move in the same direction.

---

### ROI Anchored in Behavioral Change

The confidence gains documented in this research are not abstract survey scores — they are self-reported shifts in a clinician's readiness to have a specific, high-stakes conversation. Among participants who showed gains, those gains were tied to concrete behavioral intentions: citing specific statistics in patient conversations, identifying which hesitancy type a patient has before responding, framing adverse event risk in precise rather than general terms.

For a pharmaceutical company, confidence in a product conversation translates directly to recommendation behavior. A clinician who walks into an exam room with precise data and a hesitancy-identification framework is a more effective advocate than one equipped only with general safety messaging. The game produces that clinician — and the research shows it.
